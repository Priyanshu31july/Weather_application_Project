Weather Phenomena

Tornado

A tornado is a rapidly rotating column of air that is in contact with both the surface of the Earth and a cumulonimbus cloud or, in rare cases, the base of a cumulus cloud.

Tornadoes come in many shapes and sizes, but are typically visible in the form of a condensation funnel originating from the base of a cumulonimbus cloud, with a cloud of rotating debris and dust beneath it. They are often accompanied by a loud, continuous roar or rumble, and can cause significant damage to buildings and other structures in their path.

Hurricane

A hurricane is a tropical storm with winds that have reached a constant speed of 74 mph or more. Hurricanes can cause catastrophic damage to coastlines and several hundred miles inland. They also produce torrential rains and storm surges that can cause flooding. The Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale is a 1 to 5 rating based on a hurricane's sustained wind speed.

Thunderstorm

A thunderstorm is a storm with lightning and thunder. It is produced by a cumulonimbus cloud, usually producing gusty winds, heavy rain and sometimes hail. Thunderstorms can occur year-round and at all hours. Thunderstorms are most likely to occur in the spring and summer months and during the afternoon and evening hours.

Blizzard

A blizzard is a severe snowstorm with strong winds and low visibility. Blizzards can be life-threatening if people are not prepared for the extreme cold, snow, and wind. A blizzard is classified as having sustained winds or frequent gusts of 35 mph or greater, and sufficient snow in the air to reduce visibility to less than 0.25 miles.

Tsunami

A series of ocean waves with very long wavelengths (typically several hundred kilometers) caused by large-scale disturbances of the ocean, such as earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, and landslides.
